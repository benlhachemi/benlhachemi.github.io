Instead of promoting other people's products, I wanted to build my own.

Over three years, I built more than [20 projects](/projects):

- SaaS applications
- Developer tools
- Design systems
- Desktop applications
- Automation software
- Marketplaces
- AI tools

Most failed.

Some got users.

None became the business I hoped they would be.

What I underestimated was how difficult it is to build a software business people consistently want to pay for.

And honestly, that was one of the most valuable parts of my career.

Because I learned two lessons that no tutorial can teach:

1. Building software is only one part of the equation.
2. Finding product-market fit is much harder than writing code.

### Software Engineer (2026 → Present)

After several years building my own products full-time, I've come to appreciate that the best way to grow as an engineer is to solve real problems for real people, not just my own.

I'm currently open to new opportunities: freelance projects, technical co-founder, startup roles, or product-focused engineering positions where I can bring my product-building mindset to a team that values speed, quality, and ownership.

The founder mindset hasn't disappeared.

I still enjoy exploring new ideas, experimenting with side projects, and shipping products. The difference is that these days I don't treat them as full-time bets. They are opportunities to learn, stay curious, and sharpen my craft while helping clients and teams build meaningful products.

I bring the same intensity I had when building my own companies, but now I channel it into solving problems for clients and teams who need exactly what I'm good at: turning 0 into 1.

## 🧠 My Engineering Philosophy
- **Build → Iterate → Loop:** I start with implementing ideas as simply as possible, get it to work first with minimal setup, then iterate and improve.

- **Code organization is mandatory**: A good folder structure, single responsibility, short files, clear variables, functions and class names.

- **UI/UX is a feature:** Micro-interactions, accessibility, and polish aren't "nice-to-haves", they are half of the product (or even the product in some cases). 

- **Learn by doing:** I prefer to learn by building real products over tutorials or courses.

- **AI coding agents:** IMO, every software engineer should learn how to integrate AI into their workflow. Why? Because if used properly, it will save lot of time.

## 🔁 My Workflow

1. Understand the user problem
2. Figure out all possible solutions 
3. Analyze and compare those solutions by cost, feasibility, and technical complexity
4. Build & Ship MVP
5. Measure usage, traffic, and runtime errors
6. Iterate from feedback and metrics
